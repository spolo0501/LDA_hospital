### Chapter 1ï¼ˆIntroductionï¼‰

> "Previous research has demonstrated significant differences in perceived inpatient care quality between Taiwan and the United States (Yoon & Cheng, 2021). Notably, the association between regional socioeconomic status and patient satisfaction showed **opposite patterns** in the two countries, suggesting that systemic and cultural factors play crucial roles in shaping patient experiences."

### Chapter 2.2ï¼ˆCross-Cultural Differencesï¼‰

> "Yoon and Cheng (2021) found that higher regional SES was associated with **lower** patient satisfaction in Taiwan (ORs = 0.83-0.88), while it was associated with **higher** satisfaction in the United States (ORs = 1.48-2.51). This reversal can be explained by Hofstede's cultural dimensions: Taiwan's collectivism may lead high-SES patients to have higher expectations for public healthcare, while U.S. individualism allows high-SES patients to purchase superior services through private insurance."

### Chapter 2.3ï¼ˆHealthcare Systemsï¼‰

> "The contrasting findings between Taiwan's universal healthcare system and the U.S. market-based system (Yoon & Cheng, 2021) underscore the importance of examining how different healthcare financing models influence patient satisfaction patterns."

### Chapter 5ï¼ˆConclusionsï¼‰

> "Our LDA-based analysis extends Yoon and Cheng's (2021) three-dimensional comparison by identifying **six (Taiwan) and seven (USA) distinct service quality dimensions** derived from patients' natural language reviews, providing a more comprehensive and nuanced understanding of cross-cultural differences in healthcare service expectations."
